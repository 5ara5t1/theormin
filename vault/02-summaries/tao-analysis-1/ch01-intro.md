# Tao Analysis I — Ch. 1: Introduction

## What this chapter establishes

Tao introduces analysis as "the rigorous study of such objects (real numbers, complex numbers, functions, etc), with a focus on trying to pin down precisely and accurately the qualitative and quantitative behavior of these objects". Real analysis is the systematic study of real numbers; calculus — the computational rules for manipulating functions — sits on top of real analysis as its theoretical foundation.

The text presumes that the reader has already utilized numbers, sequences, series, limits, functions, definite integrals,  and derivatives in Calc 1/2 for computation. But the true nature of these objects is still somewhat unknown. Tao's book will develop the specific theory that will describe how these objects are constructed and how properties like convergence and continuity are proven. 
Some questions posed by Tao (for more details, review CH1)
1. What is a real number? 
2. How do you take the limit of a sequence of real numbers? 
3. What is a function?

But why study analysis? Isn't it enough to just know how to compute (or just "shut up and calculate")? Knowing why something has a particular nature is much more satisfying than just knowing how to use it. Applying rules to problems without understanding why they function the way they do can also lead to misunderstanding and confusion which can be avoided. The way in which i learned calculus was somewhat rushed and hand wavy. I never received an explanation as to why convergence and continuity "worked", or at least i don't remember a very satisfying answer. 

Tao gives several examples of such problems which can arise when the rules of calculus are applied without careful consideration. 
- EX 1.2.1 (Division by Zero)
- EX 1.2.2 (Divergent Series)
- EX 1.2.3 (Divergent Sequences)
- EX 1.2.4 (Limiting Value Functions)
- EX 1.2.5 (Interchanging Sums)
- EX 1.2.6 (Interchanging Integrals)
- EX 1.2.7 (Interchanging Limits)
- EX 1.2.8 (interchanging Limits)
- EX 1.2.9 (interchanging Integrals and Limits)
- EX 1.2.10 (Interchanging Limits and Derivatives)
- EX 1.2.11 (Interchanging Derivatives)
- EX 1.2.12 (L'Hôpital's rule)
- EX 1.2.13 (Limits and lengths)
Understanding analysis will allow us to know when the application of the rules of calculus are "justified" and when they are "illegal". Understanding analysis yields an "analytical way of thinking" which is useful when working with other mathematical objects. Finally, understanding analysis yields to better mathematics in general sharpening the toolset for physics
